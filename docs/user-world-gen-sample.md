Eryndal
A world of warring kingdoms, ancient ruins, and elemental power — grand in scale, dangerous to explore.
The Aether System — Magic
Every living soul is born with an Elemental Affinity — a natural attunement to one of six forces. This affinity determines how they perceive and shape Aether, the invisible energy threaded through all living things. Aether is finite; exhaust it and you lose consciousness or worse.
Fire Water Earth Wind Lightning Void
Void is vanishingly rare — perhaps one in ten thousand. Void practitioners can unmake and nullify, making them both feared and hunted. Exceptionally talented individuals can unlock a Second Aspect — a second affinity — but no one in recorded history has mastered three.
Classes
✦
Invoker
Pure spellcasters who shape Aether externally. High power ceiling, physically fragile. The mage archetype.
⚔
Resonant
Warriors who channel Aether through their body — flaming fists, stone skin, lightning speed. Most common combat class.
◈
Arbiter
Support specialists: healers, barrier-weavers, enchanters. Underestimated by most, indispensable on any serious expedition.
◯
Null
Born without affinity — rare and often stigmatized. Cannot use elemental magic, but are immune to Aether-based effects. Some compensate with relic-tech or pure skill.
Mastery Tiers
Kindled
Newly awakened. Rough, unstable control.
Forged
Reliable and trained. Guild-eligible.
Branded
A visible Aether Mark forms on the body. Recognized across kingdoms.
Ascendant
Capable of reshaping battlefields. Named individuals at this tier are known internationally.
Sovereign
Mythic level. Fewer than a dozen alive at any time. Each one is a political event.
The Five Realms
Ashborn Empire
Fire-aspected military superpower. Expansionist, disciplined, and proud. Their Ember Legions are the most feared standing army in Eryndal. Politics are internal power struggles between noble houses competing for the Emperor's favour.
Thalassian Dominion
A confederation of coastal city-states united by trade and water-aspected culture. Fiercely independent. No standing king — governed by the Tide Council, a body of merchant admirals. The richest and most cosmopolitan region.
Verdant Sovereignty
Earth-aspected forest kingdom with a long memory. Semi-isolationist, ruled by the ancient Rootspeaker dynasty. Their territory borders every other realm, making them a quiet power broker in continental politics.
Skyward Reaches
Wind and lightning clans inhabiting the great mountain chains. Not truly unified — seven clans, frequent inter-clan skirmishes, but they close ranks instantly against outsiders. Exceptional scouts and aerial fighters.
The Fractured Wastes
Not a kingdom — the ruins of one. The collapsed heartland of the Aetheric Predecessors, now a lawless expanse of shattered cities, wandering monsters, and Abyssal Sites. No government claims it. Guilds, treasure hunters, fugitives, and the desperate all end up here eventually. The most dangerous region in Eryndal, and also the most lucrative.
Abyssal Sites — Dungeons
When the Aetheric Predecessor civilization collapsed 900 years ago, their greatest structures didn't simply crumble — they folded inward, creating self-sustaining Aether loops that generate monsters, traps, and spatial anomalies indefinitely. These are called Abyssal Sites. They cannot be permanently cleared; killing everything inside only resets them over time.
Sites are ranked by depth and estimated danger. The deeper you go, the older and stranger the Predecessor architecture becomes — and the more valuable the Relics buried within. Some Sites are mapped and partially managed. Others in the Fractured Wastes have never been fully explored.
Notable Sites
The Ashvault Beneath the Ashborn capital. Controlled access — the Empire profits from licenced dives.
The Drowned Spire Partially submerged off the Thalassian coast. Requires water-aspect or breathing relics.
The Maw Deep in the Wastes. Unranked. Multiple Ascendant-level expeditions never returned.
Relics
Predecessor-era artefacts recovered from Abyssal Sites. Most are tools or weapons with Aether properties that modern craft cannot replicate. A single quality Relic is worth more than most people will earn in a lifetime. Forgery is rampant; authentication guilds exist solely to combat this. Certain Relics are so dangerous that multiple kingdoms have treaties banning their sale.
The Delver's Compact — Guilds
The Compact is the continent-wide guild governing body — politically neutral, respected by all five realms because every realm needs Delvers to manage the Sites on their borders. Membership grants legal protections, cross-border travel rights, and access to Compact infrastructure (waypoints, infirmaries, auction houses).
Individual guilds operate under Compact licensing. Hundreds exist; most are small regional outfits. A handful are large enough to operate internationally and carry genuine political weight. Guild rank is distinct from personal mastery tier — a Branded solo practitioner might outrank an Ascendant in a poorly-regarded guild.
Trade & the Free Cities
The Amber Road is the great overland trade route connecting the Thalassian coast through the Verdant interior and up to the Skyward Reaches. It passes through a string of Free Cities — independent city-states that belong to no kingdom and survive on commerce, neutrality, and mercenary contracts.
The Free Cities are where everything and everyone mixes: merchants, Delvers between expeditions, spies, fugitives, information brokers, and Relic traders operating in legal grey areas. They are the best places to find rumours, rare gear, and morally flexible employment.
The Ancient Lore — Predecessor Civilization
Nine hundred years ago, a single civilization — the Aetheric Predecessors — ruled the entire continent from a now-vanished capital. They mastered all six elements simultaneously, built structures that defied natural law, and apparently attempted something called the Grand Convergence — a ritual to permanently fuse all six aspects into one unified force. It destroyed them in a single night. No one knows exactly what happened. The Abyssal Sites are what's left. Predecessor texts are written in a dead script that fewer than a hundred scholars in the world can partially read, and even they disagree on most translations. The prevailing theory is that the Convergence worked — and the result was Void itself.
